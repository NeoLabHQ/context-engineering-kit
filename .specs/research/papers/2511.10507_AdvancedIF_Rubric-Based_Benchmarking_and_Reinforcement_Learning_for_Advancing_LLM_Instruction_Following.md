### **1 Introduction**

Recent advances in Large Language Models (LLMs) have shown their remarkable capabilities in various
domains, including but not limited to math and code reasoning (Guo et al., 2025; Chen et al., 2021; Yang
et al., 2024; Huang and Yang, 2025), interactive tool usage (Team et al., 2025; Wang et al., 2025), and
conversational agents (Ouyang et al., 2022; Achiam et al., 2023; Comanici et al., 2025; Grattafiori et al., 2024).
Among these capabilities, instruction-following (IF) is arguably one of the most fundamental focuses of LLM
development as it underlies agents’ ability to generalize to novel tasks and translate user intent into desired
outputs (Ouyang et al., 2022; Wei et al., 2021). While contemporary LLMs can already follow direct and
simple instructions quite well, they are still struggling to deal with users’ complex instructions, which is quite
common and challenging, as the instructions may contain multiple user intentions spanned over different turns
of the conversation (He et al., 2024; Deshpande et al., 2025).


Meanwhile, Reinforcement Learning with Verifiable Rewards (RLVR) has become a powerful post-training
technique to improve LLMs’ reasoning capabilities on math and code tasks, where the model’s generations
can be easily scored by matching the final answers of math problems or unit tests for programming (Guo
et al., 2025; Yu et al., 2025; Luo et al., 2025; Lin et al., 2025). However, RLVR cannot be applied directly
to improve LLMs’ IF capabilities, as it is non-trivial to verify whether a model’s response fully follows a
user’s instructions, especially for the hard ones. While training a reward model from pairwise data (Ouyang

To further unleash the power of RL, we propose to use rubric-based RL to improve LLMs’ advanced IF
capabilities during post-training. IF tasks are naturally suitable for rubric-based RL, as complex instructions
usually can be decomposed into simpler ones, which can be verified relatively easily. In fact, rubric-based
verifiers are already deployed in challenging IF benchmarks (Zhou et al., 2023; He et al., 2024; Deshpande
et al., 2025). However, developing a scalable learning pipeline for advanced IF still faces several challenges:


  - Lack of high-quality rubrics for evaluations: Most of the existing benchmarks either have LLM-generated
prompts or LLM-generated rubrics.


  - Reliable rubric generator and verifier. Given a user prompt from the large-scale training data where
human labeling is impractical, we need a generator to synthesize corresponding rubrics to evaluate the
response, and a verifier to judge whether a response complies with the rubric. How to design a good
generator and verifier to provide reliable rubrics and judgments for training is still an open problem.


  - Reward hacking issues. Reward hacking can happen to exploit certain criteria, and we need additional
strategies to address such issues during RL.


To address these challenges, we highlight the following contributions in our work. First, we introduce
AdvancedIF, a new high-quality human-annotated benchmark for evaluating LLMs’ advanced IF capabilities:


  - **Expert-written prompts.** All prompts in AdvancedIF are carefully written by human experts. In
particular, in the multi-turn conversation category, human experts interacted with LLM to provide an

behavior and style.


  - **Expert-written rubrics.** Each prompt is paired with a rubric, which was first written by a human expert

  - **Comprehensive assessment.** AdvancedIF contains 1,600+ prompts including three important aspects of

and also shows strong performance on public benchmarks like MultiChallenge and IFEval. Our ablation
studies confirm the superiority of our finetuned rubric verifier over vanilla LLM judges and demonstrate the
effectiveness of our reward design and shaping. These strong empirical results highlight the efficacy of our
proposed RIFL pipeline, showing the promise of rubric-based RL for both training and evaluation of advanced
instruction-following capabilities of LLMs.

### **2 Related Work**


**Instruction Following in LLMs.** Instruction following is a key capability of LLMs that has been extensively
studied in recent years, including various approaches to evaluate and improve LLMs’ ability to understand
and execute human instructions. For instance, efforts in instruction tuning have shown that fine-tuning LLMs
on carefully curated sets of instructions can significantly enhance their zero-shot performance on unseen
instructions (Sanh et al., 2021; Wei et al., 2021; Chung et al., 2024). More recently, Reinforcement Learning
from Human Feedback (RLHF; Christiano et al., 2017) has been employed to align LLMs more closely with
user intent, leading to models that better adhere to instructions in practice (Stiennon et al., 2020; Ouyang
et al., 2022).


Parallel to these advancements, the evaluation of instruction-following capabilities has also seen significant


3


progress. Various benchmarks have been proposed to assess how well LLMs can follow instructions across
different contexts and complexities, using powerful LLMs as judges (Zheng et al., 2023; Dubois et al., 2024) or
verifiable rubrics as metrics (Zhou et al., 2023; He et al., 2024). While current frontier LLMs demonstrate strong
performance on many instruction-following tasks, challenges remain, particularly in handling complex, multiturn instructions or instructions that require reasoning over long contexts (He et al., 2024; Deshpande et al.,
2025), which motivates our work to further explore and address these limitations through rubric-based training
and evaluation. Our proposed benchmark, AdvancedIF, is mostly related to MultiChallenge (Deshpande et al.,
2025), which aims to evaluate LLMs’ multi-turn IF capabilities with instance-level rubrics. However, our
benchmark provides a more holistic evaluation covering broader scopes (Table 2 and Section A) and features
pure expert-written prompts and rubrics for more realistic and aligned evaluation.


**Reinforcement Learning with Human Feedback.** Reinforcement Learning with Human Feedback (RLHF) has
become a dominant paradigm to further align LLMs with human preferences after pretraining and supervised
fine-tuning. The seminal work from Ouyang et al. (2022) popularized RLHF for LLMs by introducing a
three-step process: **(a)** collecting human preference data comparing model outputs, **(b)** training a reward
model to predict these preferences, and **(c)** fine-tuning the LLM using reinforcement learning to optimize for
the learned reward signal. However, RLHF relies on large amounts of preference data and a learned reward
function that is imperfect and opaque, which can lead to issues like reward hacking (Liu et al., 2024; Bai
et al., 2022a).


To mitigate these issues, recent work has explored alternative reward signals that are more transparent and
verifiable. For instance, in domains with clear correctness criteria, such as coding and math, researchers
have leveraged programmatic success signals (e.g., passing unit tests or reaching a known answer) to train
models via reinforcement learning and significantly improve LLMs’ reasoning capabilities (Guo et al., 2025;
Yu et al., 2025; Luo et al., 2025; Gao et al., 2025; Lin et al., 2025). However, applying these methods to
general instruction following remains challenging due to the lack of automatic success criteria, motivating our
exploration of rubric-based rewards to bridge the gap between human preferences and verifiable signals.


**Rubric-based RL.** Our work is closely related to previous and concurrent work that also explores the use
of rubrics in reinforcement learning. For example, Bai et al. (2022b) propose Constitutional AI, which
uses a set of principles to guide model behavior through self-improvement without direct human feedback.
More recently, Zhou et al. (2025); Viswanathan et al. (2025) show that checklists or rubrics can be used to
generate responses for Direct Preference Optimization (DPO; Rafailov et al., 2023) or Group Relative Policy
Optimization (GRPO; Guo et al., 2025) and achieve better alignment. However, these works primarily focus
on using rubrics to generate preference data or guide model behavior indirectly, whereas our approach directly
incorporates rubric-based rewards into the reinforcement learning process to provide a more structured and
interpretable feedback signal for instruction following. Concurrently, Gunjal et al. (2025) and Huang et al.
(2025) also explore using rubric-based rewards for RL training, but they mainly focus on the synthetic rubric
generation setting with relatively small-scale LLMs on general tasks. In contrast, our work focuses on the
full-stack rubric-based training and evaluation pipeline to improve LLMs’ instruction-following capabilities at
scale, including high-quality human-annotated rubrics collection, rubric generator and verifier training, and
large-scale RLHF training with rubric-based rewards. Our experiments on LLMs of varying scales demonstrate
the effectiveness of our approach in enhancing instruction-following performance, particularly on complex
tasks that require nuanced understanding and execution of instructions.

criteria (rubric) and then leverage LLMs to check if each criterion is met by the response.

**3.1** **Benchmark Design and Collection**


**Prompts Collection.** To make sure our evaluation benchmark is of high quality, all prompts and rubrics
are created manually by human experts [1] and we ask the vendor to construct prompts with a clear focus
on instruction following. Specifically, each prompt in the data corresponds to one category of advanced
IF capabilities, such as complex instructions within single-turn conversations and multiple instructions
throughout multi-turn conversations. See Section A for a detailed description of the taxonomy. Moreover, we
avoid generating prompts that have ambiguous intentions or overlap with other domains of capabilities (e.g.,
factuality) to make sure that we concentrate on improving core IF capabilities. For multi-turn conversations,
the annotators first have a conversation with a provided LLM, and then provide instructions in the last turn.
Our prompts are collected in an adversarial way such that they are challenging enough for training – we only
keep the prompts that trigger IF failures of the model’s response in the final turn.

### **4 RIFL: Rubric-Based Instruction-Following Learning**

with advanced instruction-following capabilities. As depicted in Figure 2, our approach includes multiple
components, including rubric generator, rubric verifier, reward design and shaping. In the following subsections,
we will first formulate our problem in Section 4.1, and then illustrate each component respectively.

**4.2** **Rubric Generation**


We fine-tuned a Llama 4 Maverick model to generate rubrics for prompts. For training, we collected thousands
of human-expert annotated rubrics for a separate set of user prompts via Surge. We formatted these user
prompts and their corresponding rubrics into SFT data and trained the Llama 4 Maverick model for one
epoch. For evaluation, we used a hold-out set of human annotations. We employed an LLM to semantically
match the rubrics generated by our model with those labeled by human experts, calculating precision (the
proportion of synthetic rubrics that were also labeled by humans) and recall (the proportion of human-labeled
rubrics identified by the rubric generator). The fine-tuned model achieved an F1 score of 0.790, a significant
improvement over the starting checkpoint, which had an F1 score of 0.639.


**4.3** **Rubric Verifier Training**


RIFL requires a strong rubric verifier to provide reliable reward signals for RL. Therefore, rather than directly
prompting an off-the-shelf LLM as the rubric verifier, we propose a two-stage finetuning pipeline to train a
rubric verifier that can better align with expert human raters.


**Rubric verification data collection.** We ask the vendor to annotate a detailed rubric-based evaluation of the
generated response to each user prompt given the proposed rubric. Specifically, annotators need to go through
every rubric and decide whether it is met or not, and provide justification (i.e., chain-of-thought for the judge)
for their judgment. This golden set of evaluations, referred to as _D_ golden, is used to develop the rubric verifier
for RL training with the following two stages.


**SFT Stage.** We conduct supervised finetuning (SFT) to cold-start the model to evaluate responses based on
rubrics like expert human raters. We form a portion of _D_ golden as the SFT dataset _D_ SFT = _{_ ( **x** _j_ _,_ **y** _j_ ) _}_ _[n]_ _j_ =1 [,]
where **x** _j_ is the rubric verifier prompt including the user prompt, rubric, and model-generated response, and **y** _j_
is the human-annotated rubric-based evaluation. An example of rubric verification data is shown in Figure 3.


**RL Stage.** After SFT, we conduct a second RL stage on a broader set of rubric verifier prompts to improve our
rubric verifier’s generalization. During rollout, the verifier checks each criterion separately with a justification
for the judgment (i.e., chain-of-thought reasoning) and obtains the binary judgment, and compares this
judgment with the human expert’s binary label – YES for this criteria check passed or NO if otherwise. The
reward is calculated based on how closely the verifier’s judgment matches the expert’s judgment, measured as
the ratio of agreement between the two. Note that this is an RLVR problem as the verifier outputs are binary
signals, which we can easily match with the golden labels. The RL process is illustrated in Figure 4.

**Reward Hacking Prevention.** During the early stage of experiments, we observed some reward-hacking issues
during training. For instance, the model will generate some artifacts like “all instructions are followed” or
“this is a perfect response that meets all requirements!” in responses to mislead the rubric verifier to give
a spurious high reward. While finetuning a rubric verifier can help to mitigate this issue (Section 4.3), we
further include two additional criteria into each data point as a reward shaping technique:


  - Did the model provide a clean response without any weird artifacts such as very verbose self-evaluation?


  - Did the model provide a complete response so that the last sentence of the response is not cut off?


These criteria explicitly ask the rubric verifier to examine whether potential reward hacking exists and fully
address this issue in our experiments.

### **5 Experiments**


In this section, we present the experimental results of RIFL. We evaluate our method on benchmark datasets
and compare its performance against existing baselines. We also conduct ablation studies to understand
the contribution of each component in our method. Specifically, we aim to answer the following research
questions: **(a)** How does RIFL perform on challenging instruction-following benchmarks? **(b)** How effective is
the finetuned rubric verifier in providing reliable feedback for training compared to the vanilla LLM judge
prompted with rubrics? **(c)** What is the impact of different reward designs on the performance of RIFL? **(d)**
Does the reward shaping technique help mitigate reward hacking issues in RIFL?


**5.1** **Training and Evaluation Details**


**Training.** We use the Llama 4 Maverick model as the base policy and an internal RLHF infrastructure to
conduct the RL training. The training prompts are manually written by the vendor Surge and the rubrics
are synthetically generated by the rubric generator introduced in Section 4.2. For the rubric verifier, we also
finetune a Llama 4 Maverick model following the recipe mentioned in Section 4.3.


**Evaluation.** We evaluate the performance of RIFL and the baseline on three challenging IF benchmarks,
including our proposed AdvancedIF benchmark (Section 3.1) and two public benchmarks: IFEval (Zhou
et al., 2023) and MultiChallenge (Deshpande et al., 2025) to test both in-distribution and out-of-distribution
generalization. Specifically, IFEval is a single-turn IF benchmark with verifiable instructions, and MultiChallenge is a multi-turn IF benchmark with diverse instructions and instance-level rubrics. For AdvancedIF and
MultiChallenge, we choose o3-mini as our rubric verifier as the same as in benchmarking SOTA models in
Section 3.1. Following prior work (Zhou et al., 2023), we report the average performance of IFEval in terms of

**5.3** **Ablations on Rubric Verifier in RL**


Qualitatively, we find that the vanilla LLM judge is prone to reward hacking issues, where the model learns to
exploit the weaknesses of the judge to obtain higher rewards without truly improving its instruction-following
ability. In contrast, the finetuned rubric verifier is more robust against such issues. See Section C for a
concrete example.


**5.4** **Ablations on Reward Design**


In this section, we focus on three different reward designs for RIFL: **(a)** the default all-or-nothing reward, where
the model receives a reward of 1 only if it satisfies all criteria in the rubric, and 0 otherwise; **(a)** fractional
rubric reward, where the model receives a fractional reward based on the percentage of criteria satisfied in
the rubric; and **(c)** hybrid reward: defined as 0 _._ 5 _×_ (all-or-nothing reward) + 0 _._ 5 _×_ (fractional rubric reward).
This approach balances the strictness of the all-or-nothing criterion with the granularity of the fractional
rubric, encouraging the model to satisfy as many criteria as possible while still incentivizing complete success.
We report the performance of RIFL with these three reward designs on AdvancedIF in Table 6. We can
see that the all-or-nothing reward design achieves the best overall performance (58.1), while the fractional
rubric reward design performs the worst (53.6) and the hybrid reward design achieves a moderate performance
(55.7). These results suggest that a more stringent reward design can better incentivize the model to fully
satisfy the rubrics, leading to improved instruction following capabilities. We hypothesize that this is because
the all-or-nothing reward design encourages the model to focus on meeting all criteria in the rubric, rather
than just a subset of them, which is consistent with the instruction following benchmark evaluation protocols.
Additionally the fractional rubric reward design may introduce more noise in the reward signal as the rubric
verifier may not be perfectly accurate in evaluating each criterion individually. We leave a more comprehensive
study of reward design for future work.


**5.5** **Ablations on Reward Hacking Prevention**


We also conduct a qualitative analysis on the impact of additional criteria for reward hacking prevention
mentioned in Section 4.4. We find that these criteria can effectively mitigate reward hacking issues during
RL training. Specifically, when we remove these criteria from the training data, the model tends to generate
responses with artifacts or overlong responses to mislead the rubric verifier into giving a spurious high reward.

### **6 Conclusion**


In this paper, we propose a rubric-based learning and evaluation pipeline to improve the IF ability of LLMs. We
release AdvancedIF, a high-quality human-annotated IF benchmark to measure LLMs capability in following
complex, multi-turn instructions. We also propose a rubric-based RL pipeline RIFL, which first generates
rubrics for complex instructions, then trains a rubric verifier to provide feedback, and finally optimizes the
LLM with RL. Our experiments show that RIFL significantly improves the IF ability of LLMs. We also
conduct extensive ablation studies to validate the effectiveness of each component in our pipeline. Our work
demonstrates the potential of using rubrics as a powerful tool for training and evaluating LLMs, and opens up
new avenues for future research in this direction. We hope our work can inspire more research on improving
the instruction-following ability of LLMs, and contribute to the development of more capable and reliable AI
systems.
